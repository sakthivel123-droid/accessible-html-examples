This color solid shows how color varies along three perceptual dimensions. Brightness increases from the bottom to the top of the solid, hue changes around the solid’s perimeter, and saturation increases toward the periphery of the solid. The perception of color depends on complex blends of all three properties of light. Hue depends primarily on light wavelength, brightness on the amplitude of the light waves, and saturation on the purity of the light mixture. As a color solid demonstrates, people can perceive many different colors. Most of these variations are the result of mixing a few basic colors. There are two kinds of color mixing: subtractive and additive. Subtractive color mixing works by removing some wavelengths of light, leaving less light than was originally present. For example, you can mix yellow and blue paints to make green. Paints yield subtractive mixing because pigments absorb most wavelengths, selectively reflecting specific wavelengths that give rise to particular colors. Subtractive color mixing can also be demonstrated by stacking color filters. If you look through a sandwich of yellow and blue cellophane filters, they will block out certain wavelengths. Drag the filters together to see for yourself. Additive color mixing works by superimposing lights, putting more light in the mixture than exists in any one light by itself. To see an example, click each projector to turn it on. If you mix beams from red, green, and blue spotlights, you’ll get the additive mixture shown here.
